2024/25 | CONFIDENTIAL

Designing the future of contactless travel

Year: 2024 | Duration: 11+ months | Role: Senior UX Designer

Overview

A UK public-sector organisation needed to enhance its high-transaction digital immigration service, ensuring it met the needs of diverse global users.

Our team led the user-centred design (UCD) process, developing an innovative omni-channel prototyping approach that combined a Figma mobile prototype with a Heroku-hosted GOV.UK service prototype.

This enabled robust usability testing across multiple demographics, including different digital literacy levels, age groups, and nationalities.

Through this work, we successfully achieved a first-time GDS assessment pass, moving the service to public Beta and supporting 5+ million users worldwide.

Problem

Users needed a seamless way to prove their immigration status digitally, replacing traditional physical documents. However, key challenges included:

  • Users with limited digital skills struggled with verification.
  • Inaccessible content due to complex terminology and acronyms.
  • Technology failures leaving users unable to prove their status.
  • Users without identity documents facing barriers to account setup.

Additionally, the service needed to respond rapidly to policy shifts, such as physical documents expiring in 2024, while ensuring compliance with accessibility standards.

Solution

Our approach combined rapid prototyping, usability testing, and policy collaboration to create an inclusive digital service:

  • Developed a hybrid prototyping method integrating a Figma mobile app with a GOV.UK Heroku prototype to simulate an omni-channel experience.
  • Tested the service with diverse users across multiple countries, ensuring accessibility for different age groups, digital literacy levels, and technical constraints.
  • Streamlined content and simplified complex terminology to improve comprehension, particularly for users with limited English skills.
  • Introduced alternative verification routes, allowing users without identity documents to register through a ‘No ID’ account journey.
  • Implemented assisted digital support through partnerships with community organisations and live webchat services for users needing extra help.

Process

  1. User research: Conducted usability testing with international users to understand barriers to proving their immigration status. Engaged with community organisations to assess assisted digital needs.
  2. Prototyping and iteration: Designed and iterated on Figma-based mobile flows for app interactions. Developed a Heroku-hosted GOV.UK prototype for service testing. Ensured realistic policy-driven testing scenarios, allowing adjustments based on government regulations.
  3. Accessibility and inclusion enhancements: Added a ‘skip face scan’ option for users with conditions like photo-sensitive epilepsy. Allowed expired immigration status evidence to be used for up to 18 months to prevent legal uncertainty due to technical failures.
  4. Collaboration and policy alignment: Worked closely with policy teams, business ops, product owners, analysts, and developers to align service design with government legislation and operational feasibility.

Results

We achieved a first-time GDS assessment pass, allowing the service to move to public Beta.

  • 5+ million users worldwide can now prove their immigration status digitally
  • Simplified content and removal of complex acronyms through inclusive language design
  • Assisted digital support for users who cannot access online services independently

Conclusion

By adopting a user-centred, omni-channel design approach, we successfully improved the digital immigration status service, ensuring it works for all users, regardless of digital ability or access to technology.

My hybrid Figma + GOV.UK prototyping method allowed rapid iteration, enabling the team to respond swiftly to policy shifts and real-world challenges.

This approach not only improved usability but also set a precedent for future UK GOV digital transformation projects, proving the value of blending policy, technology, and user research to deliver accessible, inclusive services.

Next steps

  • Further enhancements to support ‘No ID’ users, including a video verification route for those unable to provide traditional identification.
  • Grant funding for community organisations, allowing local groups to assist users with eVisa applications.
  • Exploration of additional support models, such as expanding assisted digital webchat services and partnerships with trusted organisations to provide in-person verification support.
